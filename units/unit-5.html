Portfolio Compilation

Overview

A portfolio is a curated collection of your best work, organised so that someone else can clearly understand your process and skills. Your Phase 1 portfolio documents the full journey from research to working prototype and serves as your primary evidence for ESC and ICT assessment.

Portfolio Structure (Word or OneNote)

Your portfolio must follow this structure. Each section has a required minimum:

Section 1: Introduction

Game title, genre, chosen engine, target platform. One paragraph describing your game concept as it stands now (it may have evolved from your original pitch — that's fine, note the changes).

Section 2: Research & Design (Unit 1–2 evidence)

Excerpts from your Genre Research Report (Task 2.1) and Game Design Document (Task 2.3). Not the full documents — select the most relevant 1–2 pages from each. Include your original Gantt chart and an annotated version showing actual vs planned progress.

Section 3: Asset Production (Unit 3 evidence)

4–6 asset samples (screenshots of your best sprites, tileset, and audio waveforms in Audition). Your Asset Catalogue table (Task 3.4) showing copyright compliance for all assets.

Section 4: Game Development (Unit 4 evidence)

3–4 screenshots of your prototype in action. One annotated code/script screenshot (from Task 4.2) explaining your core mechanic. Your self-test checklist (Task 4.3) showing the prototype vs GDD comparison.

Section 5: Digital Ethics & Safety (ESC C4 · ICT C2)

A dedicated section (300–400 words) summarising how you applied ethical and safe digital practice throughout Phase 1. Cover: OHS compliance, copyright decisions, social media safety (e.g. how you'd share your game safely), and any academic integrity decisions (how you cited sources, how you avoided plagiarism).

Section 6: Reflection (ICT C5)

400–500 words covering: (a) skills you're most proud of developing, (b) the biggest challenge of Phase 1 and how you overcame it, (c) where your prototype currently sits vs your original GDD vision, and (d) what you'd do differently if starting over.

Submission Checklist

  • All 6 sections present with correct structure and minimum content
  • Section 5 (Ethics): covers OHS, copyright, social media safety, academic integrity
  • Section 6 (Reflection): 400–500 words addressing all four prompts
  • Asset samples: 4–6 images showing variety across sprites, tileset, audio
  • Asset Catalogue table included with all required fields completed
  • Annotated code screenshot included and explained in plain English
  • Gantt chart: original + annotated actual-vs-planned version
  • Saved as Task5.1_Portfolio_Firstname_Lastname.docx or exported as .pdf

5.2

Final Presentation & Prototype Demo

ESC C3 — Follow presentation specification; review via feedback ICT C3 — Present ideas clearly; address audience needs ICT C5 — Reflect on progress; plan future actions
⏱ 1 lesson prep + presentation session  ·  🛠 PowerPoint or Adobe Express, engine  ·  📅 Weeks 21–22
▾

Overview

Game developers present their work constantly — to publishers, playtesters, teammates, and at events like PAX, GDC, and local game jams. This presentation is your first professional demo: live gameplay, explained design decisions, honest reflection on what worked and what didn't.

Presentation Requirements (5–7 minutes)

  • Slide 1: Game title, genre, engine, your name
  • Slides 2–3: Game concept — what is your game? Who is it for? (Reference your research)
  • Live demo: Play your prototype for 2–3 minutes. Narrate what the player is doing and what mechanics are in play. If something goes wrong, stay calm and explain what should have happened.
  • Slide 4–5: Design decisions — pick 2 decisions from your GDD and explain why you made them (e.g. "I chose pixel art because..." "I chose GMS2 instead of Unity because...")
  • Slide 6: What I'd do differently — be honest. One thing that didn't work and what you'd change.
  • Q&A: Answer at least 2 questions from your audience. It's fine to say "I don't know yet — that's something I'd like to figure out in Unit 6."

You may use PowerPoint (for structured slides) or Adobe Express (for a more visual, designed presentation). Maximum 8 slides.

After the Presentation — Feedback Response Document

Collect written feedback from at least 3 audience members using the peer feedback form. Then produce a short Word document (200–300 words) titled "Feedback Received & My Response" covering:

  • Summarise the 3 main themes from the feedback you received
  • For each theme: will you act on it in Unit 6? How?
  • One piece of feedback that surprised you and why

Submission Checklist

  • Slide deck (max 8 slides): all 6 required content areas covered
  • Live demo delivered: prototype shown running (even if imperfect)
  • Design decisions section: 2 decisions explained with reasoning (not just what — why)
  • Q&A: at least 2 questions answered
  • Feedback Response document: 200–300 words, 3 themes addressed, surprise feedback noted
  • Submitted: Task5.2_Slides_Firstname_Lastname.pptx + Task5.2_FeedbackResponse_Firstname.docx
